Overview

media.games surfaces what the media isn't telling you and what it's distracting you with — by detecting omissions in coverage of stories that matter and saturation of stories that don't.

What is media.games?

media.games is a platform that monitors news coverage across dozens of major sources to surface what the media is and isn't covering. We don't fact-check. We don't rate bias. We observe coverage patterns in aggregate and detect anomalies — stories that are being ignored, amplified, or covered in surprising ways.

The platform works in three stages:

  1. Collect — We ingest articles from 28 global news sources and group them into stories.
  2. Evaluate — Each story is assessed against transparent, auditable public interest criteria using an LLM-based evaluator.
  3. Detect — We compare coverage patterns across sources to identify anomalies — gaps, amplifications, and distortions.
Our principle
We focus on systems, not individuals. The platform prioritizes institutional behavior over individual incidents. This avoids distortion from cherry-picked crime stories, culture-war outrage content, and celebrity controversies.

Aggregating the News

From raw articles to organized stories — how data flows through the platform.

Sources

We collect articles from 28 news sources spanning US domestic, international, left-leaning, right-leaning, and centrist perspectives. The full list is available on the Sources page.

Articles are ingested continuously via NewsAPI.ai, which provides headline, snippet, body text, source, and publication time for each article.

Scope

Not every article enters the pipeline. We filter to in-scope content: articles covering politics, business, health, environment, science, technology, and general news. Sports, entertainment, and lifestyle content is classified as out-of-scope.

How Stories Are Grouped

A single news event is typically covered by many sources. Rather than treating each article independently, we group articles about the same event into a cluster.

Clustering works by converting each article's text into a numerical representation (an embedding) and grouping articles whose representations are highly similar. This lets us see the same story across many sources simultaneously.

Why this matters
Clustering is what makes anomaly detection possible. Without it, we'd just have a list of articles. With it, we can ask: "Which sources covered this story? Which didn't? How much attention did each give it?"

Evaluating Public Interest

How we determine whether a story matters to the public — transparently and reproducibly.

Why evaluate public interest?

To detect anomalies in coverage, we need a consistent way to distinguish stories that matter from noise. A celebrity breakup getting 200 articles isn't an anomaly — it's entertainment. A regulatory change affecting millions getting 3 articles might be.

We use an LLM-based evaluator that assesses each story against four binary criteria. The evaluation is deterministic, reproducible, and auditable — the prompt is versioned, the model is recorded, and every result is stored.

The Four Criteria

1 Real-World Impact

Does the story describe events that materially affect people's safety, health, economic conditions, civil rights, or security?

2 Institutional Action

Does the story focus on actions or decisions by governments, courts, regulators, legislatures, large corporations, or security forces?

3 Widespread Impact

Does the issue affect large populations or systems, rather than a small number of individuals?

4 New Information

Does the story report a new development, decision, event, or verified fact not previously publicly reported?

The Gate Rule

A story qualifies as public interest when it meets at least 3 of the 4 criteria, and at least one of those must be an anchor criterion — either Real-World Impact or Institutional Action.

// A story qualifies as public interest when:
criteria_met >= 3
AND (real_world_impact = true OR institutional_action = true)

Public Interest Classifications

Label Criteria Met Public Interest?
High 4 of 4 Yes
Moderate 3 of 4 (with anchor) Yes
Low 2 of 4 No
Not Public Interest 0–1 of 4 No
Transparency
Every evaluation stores the full reasoning, the prompt version used, and the model version. This means any result can be audited or re-evaluated.

Geographic Relevance Classifications

In addition to public interest, each story is tagged with geographic relevance — a set of independent boolean flags evaluated by the same LLM alongside the four PI criteria. These flags are independent of the PI gate rule — a story can be nationally relevant without being public interest, and vice versa.

FlagEvaluates
global_relevance Does the story affect multiple countries, international systems, or have consequences beyond any single nation's borders?
US_relevance Does the story directly affect U.S. governance, economy, security, or population, or involve U.S. institutions or citizens?
state_local_relevance Does the story primarily affect a specific U.S. state, city, or locality rather than the nation as a whole?
Why geographic relevance?
The public interest evaluation has no geographic dimension. Without these flags, foreign-only stories (e.g. UK local politics) that score high on PI would distort anomaly detection — particularly detectors like Domestic Blindspot that specifically look for gaps in US coverage. Geographic relevance lets us distinguish globally-relevant PI from US-specific PI from locally-scoped stories.

Detecting Anomalies

Surfacing the stories the media is missing, amplifying, or covering in surprising patterns.

What is an anomaly?

An anomaly is a pattern in news coverage that deviates from what you'd expect. High public interest stories that barely get covered. Low-value stories that dominate the news cycle. Stories covered heavily by international sources but ignored domestically.

media.games uses a growing library of anomaly detectors — each one looks for a specific type of coverage pattern. When a detector fires, it surfaces the story along with the evidence: which sources covered it, how many articles, and what the public interest score was.

For the full list of detectors and their definitions, see Anomalies under Key Concepts.

Growing over time
The anomaly detection system is designed to be extensible. New detectors will be added as we identify additional coverage patterns worth surfacing.

Public Interest

A story is "public interest" if it helps the public understand how power is exercised, how institutions affect people's lives, or what new facts about those systems have emerged.

What qualifies

  • War, economic shocks, public health crises, environmental disasters
  • Court rulings, legislation, regulatory enforcement, institutional failures
  • Major policy changes affecting large populations
  • New evidence, documents, or investigative revelations

What doesn't qualify

  • Celebrity activity, lifestyle, consumer products
  • Random crime, interpersonal disputes
  • Commentary that doesn't analyze real institutional actions
  • Historical retrospectives (unless revealing new evidence)
  • Reaction pieces or follow-ups repeating known facts

For the full evaluation process, see Evaluating Public Interest.

Coverage Asymmetry

How we measure the attention a story receives — and where that attention is uneven.

Important
These metrics are observations, not judgments. Asymmetry doesn't mean anyone is right or wrong — it means coverage is uneven, which is worth noticing.

Metrics

MetricDefinition
article_countTotal articles about a story across all sources
news_to_noise_ratioRatio of public interest articles to total articles (0.0 to 1.0). A high ratio means substantive coverage; a low ratio means filler.
pol_skewPolitical lean skew: (left_count - right_count) / (left_count + right_count). Ranges from -1.0 (only right sources) to +1.0 (only left sources). Center sources are excluded.
geo_skewGeographic skew: (us_count - nonus_count) / (us_count + nonus_count). Ranges from -1.0 (only non-US sources) to +1.0 (only US sources).

Political Lean Skew

pol_skew measures whether a story is being covered asymmetrically along political lines. It compares article counts from left-leaning sources against right-leaning sources, excluding center sources from the calculation.

A pol_skew near 0 means balanced coverage across the political spectrum. A value near +1.0 means the story is being covered almost exclusively by left-leaning sources. A value near -1.0 means almost exclusively by right-leaning sources. Large skew in either direction is interesting — it may indicate that the story is being emphasized or de-emphasized along political lines.

Geographic Skew

geo_skew measures whether a story is being covered asymmetrically between US and international sources.

A value near +1.0 means the story is covered almost exclusively by US sources. A value near -1.0 means almost exclusively by non-US sources. Stories with extreme negative geo skew — high international coverage but low US coverage — may represent domestic blindspots.

News-to-Noise Ratio

The news-to-noise ratio indicates how much of a source's coverage of a story is substantive versus filler. A ratio of 1.0 means every article is public interest. A ratio of 0.0 means none are.

Stories & Clusters

On media.games, a "story" isn't a single article — it's a cluster of articles about the same event.

How it works

When multiple sources report on the same event, their articles are grouped into a cluster. Each cluster represents one real-world story and contains every article covering it, regardless of source.

Why it matters

Clustering is what makes it possible to compare coverage across sources. It lets us answer questions like: "Did Fox News cover this story? How about the BBC? How many articles did each publish?"

Cluster size

The cluster size is the total number of articles grouped into a topic. A cluster with 150 articles is a major story getting broad coverage. A cluster with 3 articles is a niche story — or a potential omission.

Anomalies

Patterns in coverage that deviate from what you'd expect — and what each detector looks for.

What is an anomaly?

An anomaly is a pattern in news coverage that deviates from what you'd expect. High public interest stories that barely get covered. Low-value stories that dominate the news cycle. Stories covered heavily by international sources but ignored domestically.

media.games uses a growing library of anomaly detectors — each one looks for a specific type of coverage pattern. When a detector fires, it surfaces the story along with the evidence: which sources covered it, how many articles, and what the public interest score was.

Detectors

Domestic Blindspot

US story + public interest + zero US sources

A story that is relevant to the US public interest but is not being covered by any US-based source. Only international sources are reporting on it. This is the most extreme form of coverage gap — the audience most affected by the story has no domestic media covering it.

Omission

high public interest + low coverage

A story that scores high on public interest criteria but has very few articles covering it across all sources. These are important stories that the media ecosystem is largely ignoring.

Distraction

low public interest + high coverage

A story that does not meet public interest criteria but is receiving heavy coverage anyway. This pattern suggests media resources and audience attention are being consumed by stories with limited civic value.

Clusterbomb

low PI story + high coverage + inside a high PI cluster

A low public interest story that surges in coverage within a cluster that otherwise contains high public interest content. This can dilute attention from the substantive stories in the same topic area — like a celebrity trial drowning out coverage of judicial reform in the same "courts" cluster.

Extensible
This is the initial set of detectors. New detectors will be added over time as we identify additional coverage patterns worth monitoring. Each will be documented here with its definition and detection logic.

Bias Score

The bias score is a composite rating calculated by normalizing and averaging the bias assessments from three independent media monitoring organizations. Each organization uses its own methodology, scale, and editorial process to rate outlets — we normalize their scores to a common range, average them, and convert back to produce a single label.

We don't produce our own bias ratings. The composite exists solely to provide a consistent grouping for coverage analysis — it determines which political group each source belongs to for calculating metrics like pol_skew.

Composite calculation
Each score is normalized to a [-1, 1] range by dividing by the scale maximum (6, 42, and 10 respectively). The three normalized scores are averaged, then converted back to the AllSides scale (× 6). The resulting composite uses AllSides label thresholds: Left (≤ -3), Lean Left (-3 to -1), Center (-1 to 1), Lean Right (1 to 3), Right (≥ 3).

Methodology

Deep dives into how each system works, for those who want full transparency.

Public Interest Evaluation — Full Specification

Each story is evaluated by an LLM (currently GPT-4o-mini) against four binary criteria. The prompt is versioned, temperature is set to 0 for determinism, and every evaluation stores the full reasoning, model version, and prompt version.

Criteria:

  1. material_impact — Does the story materially affect safety, health, economics, rights, or security?
  2. institutional_action — Does it focus on actions by governments, courts, regulators, large corporations?
  3. scope_scale — Does it affect large populations or systems?
  4. new_information — Does it report something new, not previously public?

Gate rule: met_count >= 3 AND (material_impact OR institutional_action)

Commentary and opinion only qualify if they analyze real institutional actions. Historical stories fail unless revealing new evidence.

Bias Data Sources

We don't rate bias ourselves. We use three independent external sources as metadata on sources, which enables coverage gap analysis across political groups.

Sources and scales:

  • AllSides — Bias meter, scale of -6 to +6
  • Ad Fontes Media — Bias scale of -42 to +42, plus a reliability score
  • Media Bias Fact Check — Bias scale of -10 to +10, plus a factual rating

Composite calculation: Each score is normalized to [-1, 1], the three are averaged, then converted back to the AllSides scale. Labels follow AllSides thresholds: Left (≤ -3), Lean Left (-3 to -1), Center (-1 to 1), Lean Right (1 to 3), Right (≥ 3).

Clustering Approach

Articles are converted to vector embeddings and grouped by semantic similarity. Articles whose embeddings fall within a similarity threshold are assigned to the same cluster. Each cluster represents a single real-world story or event.

Cluster quality is validated using a heatmap that shows the topic-by-source matrix, allowing manual inspection of whether grouped articles genuinely belong together.

Limitations & Known Gaps

We believe in being transparent about what the system doesn't do well:

  • Coverage is limited to 28 English-language text sources — many perspectives are not represented
  • Video news is not currently represented. Sources like cable news broadcasts, YouTube news channels, and video-first platforms are not included in the pipeline. Coverage analysis is based exclusively on published text articles.
  • LLM-based public interest evaluation, while reproducible, reflects the biases of the model
  • Clustering can occasionally merge distinct-but-related stories or split a single story across clusters
  • Article volume is a proxy for editorial attention, but doesn't capture placement, length, or tone
  • Bias ratings from external sources may lag behind editorial shifts at sources
